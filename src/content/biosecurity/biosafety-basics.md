# An Introduction to Biosafety Levels

Biosafety levels (BSLs) are a classification system used to describe the
containment precautions required when working with biological agents. The
system ranges from BSL-1 (minimal risk) to BSL-4 (highest risk), each
corresponding to a progressively more dangerous class of pathogen.

## The Four Levels

**BSL-1** applies to agents not known to cause disease in healthy adults.
Standard microbiological practices apply; no special containment equipment
is required beyond basic lab hygiene.

**BSL-2** covers agents that present moderate hazard to personnel and the
environment. Access to the lab is restricted, and protective equipment such
as gloves and face protection is required.

**BSL-3** is used for indigenous or exotic agents with potential for
respiratory transmission and which may cause serious or lethal disease.
All work is performed in a biosafety cabinet; the lab has specialized
ventilation and access controls.

**BSL-4** is reserved for dangerous and exotic agents that pose a high risk
of life-threatening disease for which no vaccine or therapy is available.
Full-body pressure suits or class III biological safety cabinets are required.

## Why It Matters

Understanding BSLs is foundational to biosecurity policy analysis. Debates
over dual-use research of concern (DURC), gain-of-function experiments, and
laboratory biosafety regulations all hinge on accurate classification of the
agents involved.
